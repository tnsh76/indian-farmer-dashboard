      Reliable floor vs. lottery ticket: how consistent is each format?

      Video count by view-count bracket. Long-form spreads across the whole range with 89 videos stuck under 5K views; Shorts have almost no videos below 5K but a real shot at 1M+.

      So what: Shorts effectively guarantee a view floor — the algorithm gives nearly every Short some minimum distribution — while long-form is a much wider gamble: 89 long-form videos (38% of all long-form) landed under 5K views. If the goal is dependable reach, Shorts are the lower-risk bet; if the goal is a rare breakout, both formats can go viral, but long-form's downside is far more common.

      Posting cadence vs. views generated

      Videos published inside the 90-day window, grouped by week. More uploads did not reliably mean more views.

      So what: the week of Feb 9 posted 5 videos and pulled 2.8M views; the week of Jan 26 posted more videos (6) but only drew 827K. Cadence alone doesn't predict performance — this argues against a "post more, worry later" schedule and for a smaller number of higher-conviction uploads per week.

      Best day to publish

      Average views by weekday of publish, across the full video catalog (not just the 90-day window).

      So what: Thursday uploads average 135K views — over 2.5x Monday's 53K — despite Friday being by far the most-used publish day (173 videos). The channel is heaviest on the day that performs worst on average. Shifting even a portion of Friday's volume to Wednesday/Thursday is a free reach gain, no new content required.

      Long-form: is there a sweet spot for length?

      Long-form videos only, bucketed by duration. Retention (% of runtime watched) falls steadily as videos get longer — but average views rise.

      So what: retention rate and raw views move in opposite directions as videos get longer — 25+ minute videos hold only 17% of their runtime on average but still pull the most views per video, likely because in-depth "A to Z" guides get more search/browse distribution. Don't chase retention % alone as a length signal; a shorter video with higher retention isn't necessarily outperforming a longer one on total reach.

      Data cleaning notes

      • A summary "Total" row was mixed into the per-video table — excluded from all video-level breakdowns and used only for channel-wide KPIs.
      • The per-video list (498 rows) sums to ~43.6M views, about 2.8% below the reported channel total of 44.88M — this export appears to exclude a long tail of very-low-view videos, so video-level splits reflect ~97% of total views, not 100%.
      • 2 videos show a negative net subscriber count (more unsubscribes than subscribes while watching) — kept as-is, a valid YouTube metric, not an error.
      • 1 video has 0 recorded impressions, making CTR mathematically undefined — left blank rather than defaulted to zero.
      • Impressions/CTR are only reported by YouTube for 5 of 19 traffic sources — structurally blank, not missing data.
      • The "Chart data" tab tracks daily trends for only 5 individual videos; the "Totals" tab has only a daily average-view-duration series — day-by-day trending below the whole-channel level is limited by the source export.
      • The brief mentions a "Courses platform" — no such data exists in this file; course activity was inferred from the 4 videos whose titles reference the course.
